The most impressive sights in Egypt are the ones that must be seen in person. Not the pyramids, or tombs, or mummies, but the ancient temples and colossi. 

I just recovered from the flu, which started the day of our 24 hours trip from Luxor back home to Los Angeles. Normally, I don't mind a ten hour flight. But after a day sightseeing in Luxor, two hours in Luxor airport security, four hours at Cairo airport, four hours of fit-full sleep to Paris, the then rapid onset of flu symptoms, four more hours at Charles de Gaulle - the ten hours to LAX felt like twenty. So I've been slow to organize the photos from Egypt.

We were invited to a three day wedding in Cairo. We spent a week in the capital and two nights in Luxor. The wedding was a blast. And Cairo is a developing city struggling with overpopulation and underinvestment, with all the usual problems (or personality) that come with that. But the shocking levels of air pollution took us by surprise, forming an unescapable holiday backdrop.

In Cairo, we completed the typical tourist to-dos - the Pyramids and the Sphinx, the royal mummies (National Museum of Egyptian Civilization), and Coptic (pre-Islam Egyptian Christianity) quarter. Sadly we only had time for one mosque. We sampled the fava bean cuisine (foul, falafels), Koshary, and other decent Egyptian fare. We ignored warnings and bought stuff at the bazaar. And we saw King Tut's treasures at the brand new, expansive billion dollar GEM (Grand Egyptian Museum). With the exception of King Tut's treasures - which can only be fully appreciated as a single complete set, I think everything else can be appreciated from afar, from National Geographic or The British Museum.

Everybody knows the Pyramids. Who built them? How? What secrets do they keep? These questions have captured everyone's imagination throughout the ages. Standing in their shadow, the scale of their scope and ambition does make one ponder his place in the sand. An undeniable peak in epic ancient engineering and coordination, the Pyramids were deemed a failure in their original purpose nearly as soon as they were completed. Turns out it's not a good idea to conspicuously advertise the location of your secret treasure cache meant for your eternal afterlife. Later pharaohs would opt for the construction of underground tombs in the Valley of the Kings (in Luxor, not Cairo) to manage costs and looting.

The Pyramids have endured by virtue of their size, yet lack resemblance to their ancient selves. Their tunnels and crypts probably bare and empty by the time of Alexander the Great. Their surroundings vandalized by generations of conquerors, travelers, and tourists today. 

At the Valley of the Kings in Luxor, the royal tombs buried underground suffer a similar fate. A significant amount of carved wall hieroglyphs are intact, with many retaining a measure of beautiful faded color. This alone makes a trip worth it. But most tombs have been open and looted since ancient times. With a single untouched and relatively minuscule tomb - King Tut's, being accidentally discovered in modern times, producing the hoard that takes up half of the space at GEM. We are left today with a tiny fraction of what ancient Egyptians celebrated as peak funerary art. 

Luxor is known for its temples. The name 'Luxor' was derived from the name given by Arabic conquerers of the ancient city of Thebes - meaning 'the palaces'. The new inhabitants mistook the huge ancient Egyptian temples for palaces. In addition to the Valley of the Kings, the Valley of the Queens, and the Luxor Museum (which also feels ancient), we saw the temples at Karnak, Luxor Temple, Habu Temple, Hatshepsut Temple, Hathor Temple (Deir el-Medina) and the Colossi of Memnon.

These temples and the many countless statues, built from carved sandstone blocks, granite and alabaster, are truly a sight to be a seen. Instantly transporting one thousands of years in time. The colossal scenes depicting kingly offerings, the meticulous carvings documenting offerings or spoils of war, the attention to symmetry across cavernous halls, the detailed handiwork depicting animals, stars, and daily life. All of it presented in a way resembling how its builders intended. All of it, until relatively recently, buried by centuries of sand, water, and soil.

The realization that all the temples and statues standing before us were at some point destroyed by earthquakes, buried, re-discovered, and meticulously rebuilt piece by piece, like a colossal jigsaw puzzle, to be finally enjoyed by us - that's why I recommend visiting Luxor. Standing among the hundred gigantic columns in the Hypostyle Hall at Karnak, one can fully experience the vision that the builders had in mind for visitors. 

Separated by 3,500 years, the artisans and engineers of ancient Egypt are united with today's archaeologists and laborers, in the ongoing endeavor to preserve in eternal glory their pharaohs and gods. These temples, while buried, have endured.
